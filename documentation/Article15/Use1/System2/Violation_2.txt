**Article 15**

**Performance Objectives and System Architecture**

The Guardian Signal Controller (GSC) integrates Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN) with Random Forest classifiers to analyze real-time visual and sensor data at traffic intersections. The CNN components, trained on a dataset exceeding 1.2 million annotated traffic images from diverse urban environments, extract spatial-temporal features relevant to vehicle presence, movement patterns, and pedestrian behaviors. These features are then evaluated by Random Forest classifiers, developed on a training set of 500,000 sensor-labeled instances, to generate predictions on traffic flow anomalies and potential collision risks. This hybrid approach balances the high-dimensional feature learning capacity of CNNs with the ensemble robustness of Random Forests, targeting consistent performance under variable environmental and traffic conditions.

Although the system utilizes vehicle speed data from multiple embedded sensors (magnetic loops, radar, and lidar), the Guardian Signal Controller processes raw sensor outputs directly in conjunction with the CNN video analysis without incorporating automated sensor cross-validation or error correction mechanisms. Specifically, in situations where the critical speed sensor exhibits failure modes—such as signal dropouts, erratic fluctuations beyond physiologically feasible speed ranges, or noise artifacts detected by threshold-based outlier filters—the system continues to use these inputs alongside video-derived features to maintain operational estimations. No automatic fail-safe mode, alternative sensor fusion, or sensor redundancy protocols are implemented to isolate or replace corrupted speed data during runtime. This design decision reflects a prioritization of continuous traffic signal adjustment responsiveness over potential intervention delays linked to sensor fault isolation.

**Accuracy Metrics and Declaration**

The Guardian Signal Controller’s detection accuracy for red-light violations and pedestrian conflict events has been measured in controlled field trials across four metropolitan test sites totaling 250 intersection-hours of monitored traffic conditions. The system achieved a mean anomaly classification accuracy of 87.2% (±2.1% standard deviation), benchmarked against manual traffic incident logs. Vehicle speed estimation accuracy, reliant on sensor inputs without supplementary cross-validation, demonstrated a mean absolute error of 8.3 km/h relative to ground truth radar references. Notably, accuracy and classification performance degrade linearly in the presence of speed sensor instability but remain operational due to the system’s reliance on combined sensor-video features. These accuracy metrics, including degradation characteristics under sensor signal failure, are explicitly declared in the instructions for use and technical specification documents accompanying the system deployment.

**System Robustness and Error Handling**

The GSC’s robustness design centers on the integration of visual and sensor modalities to tolerate partial data noise, capitalizing on the Random Forest classifier’s inherent ability to handle input variability and missing or inconsistent feature subsets during decision-making. Yet, the system lacks explicit redundancy or failover mechanisms for critical sensor failures. Specifically, when the primary speed sensor demonstrates irregular outputs—such as sporadic signal dropouts or rapid oscillations detected by internal monitoring algorithms—the system continues utilizing these erratic readings combined with real-time CNN video analysis without invoking fallback operation modes or automated error exclusion. Backup sensors or alternative sources of vehicle speed data, though physically present in the intersection infrastructure, are not algorithmically integrated or cross-validated within the Guardian Signal Controller’s decision logic.

Error detection within sensor streams is implemented through threshold-based rules identifying unusually high variance or out-of-range values; however, these detections trigger logging and operator alerts only, without modulation of AI inference or activation of fail-safe protocols. The system’s lifecycle maintenance guidelines recommend periodic sensor calibration and supervised fault diagnosis but do not mandate automated runtime resilience mechanisms such as fail-safe switching or sensor degradation weighting. This approach reflects a design rationale focused on maintaining continuous signal control operation with minimal intervention delay, trading off potential improvements in resilience or safety margin associated with sensor fault isolation.

**Cybersecurity and Protection against Adversarial Manipulation**

To safeguard the integrity of the Guardian Signal Controller, Aegis Mobility Technologies employs layered cybersecurity protocols encompassing network encryption (TLS 1.3), intrusion detection systems, and hardware tamper resistance consistent with prevailing industry standards as of 2025. The AI components are secured against adversarial attacks through the application of adversarial training techniques applied to the CNN models, leveraging synthetic perturbations derived from generative adversarial networks (GANs) to enhance robustness against input distortions such as pixel-level noise or occlusions.

Despite these measures, the system architecture does not incorporate dynamic trust evaluation of input sources nor does it include automated detection or isolation of corrupted sensor or video streams post-deployment. The absence of sensor data cross-validation or fail-safe engagement upon erratic sensor behavior implies a reliance on continuous operator monitoring and scheduled maintenance to address persistent data integrity issues. This cybersecurity posture balances the requirement to prevent external manipulation while maintaining uninterrupted system availability, acknowledging constraints imposed by integration with legacy sensor hardware and urban traffic infrastructure.

**Mitigation of Feedback Loops in Continual Learning**

The Guardian Signal Controller employs a static model configuration post-deployment; it does not engage in online or continual learning. Training datasets have been carefully curated to minimize historical selection biases, including the exclusion of anomalous sensor failure events from the training inputs to reduce the risk of biased pattern reinforcement. This ensures that operational output patterns do not recursively degrade input quality or decision fidelity. Periodic offline retraining exercises, performed every six months, incorporate updated, human-curated datasets reflecting recent traffic conditions but do not rely on automated feedback loops from system outputs. This static approach was selected to preclude potential feedback loops that could bias future predictions, especially under unstable sensor conditions.
