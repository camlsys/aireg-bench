**Article 14**

**Design for Effective Human Oversight During Use**  
The Recruitment Decision Forest system is architected to present a clear, straightforward interface for recruiters that prominently displays candidate scores and rankings derived from Gradient Boosted Decision Tree (GBDT) ensembles trained on structured data including candidate demographics, application responses, and historical hiring outcomes. Recruiter interface design intentionally prioritizes usability and efficiency by providing direct access to ranked candidate lists without embedding additional contextual disclaimers or uncertainty prompts. This enables human reviewers to quickly assimilate model outputs and make expedited decisions without procedural interruptions. The system’s runtime environment supports continuous access to ranked results but does not incorporate explicit human-machine interaction mechanisms such as confidence visualizations or uncertainty indicators, aligning with the design rationale of minimizing friction in recruiter workflows.

**Mitigation of Risks to Health, Safety, and Fundamental Rights Through Human Oversight**  
Recognizing potential impacts on applicants’ fundamental rights stemming from recruitment decisions, human oversight mechanisms are implemented primarily through recruiter interaction with ranked candidate lists that reflect model-learned patterns. The system’s design relies on human decision-makers exercising judgment in accepting candidate recommendations. Given the system’s operational context—initial candidate screening to inform recruiter choices rather than final hiring decisions—it assumes that human users are positioned to address possible false positives or negatives by reviewing additional candidate information outside the AI outputs if deemed necessary. No automated locks or forced critical review steps intervene; this approach aligns with the intended use parameters where recruiters are empowered to leverage model rankings as default recommendations, with the opportunity for discretionary action.

**Risk-Based and Contextual Oversight Measures Realized by Provider Decisions**  
In accordance with risk calibration and system autonomy level, the provider has embedded measures into the system that facilitate recruiter interaction by delivering deterministic, stable candidate score outputs generated by an ensemble GBDT model trained on a labeled dataset of approximately 75,000 anonymized past recruitment records spanning multiple industrial sectors and job levels. The model’s feature importance metrics and decision paths are accessible internally for auditing purposes but are not exposed in real-time to end users. Provider-implemented safeguards include automated data validation pipelines ensuring input feature consistency and retraining schedules quarterly to accommodate shifts in hiring criteria or data drift, thereby indirectly supporting oversight by maintaining model performance integrity. No direct deployer-implemented oversight measures related to user interface design or recruiter workflow enforcement are mandated or recommended by the provider, consistent with the compliance profile favoring default acceptance without imposed review steps.

**Enabling Recruiters to Understand, Monitor, and Control System Operation**  
The system is delivered with technical documentation that details the AI’s algorithmic approach, training dataset characteristics, and known model limitations, aimed at recruiter managers and system administrators rather than front-line users. Recruiter-facing software components emphasize transparency through clearly ranked candidate lists without accompanying interpretive or uncertainty information accessible during routine use. Recruiters are provided with a ‘stop’ function allowing them to pause or halt candidate scoring processes where necessary (e.g., if system errors or interruptions occur), ensuring procedural control and safe operational states. However, no interface element encourages overriding or disregarding AI output on an individual candidate basis; recruiters may implicitly decide to exclude candidates by not advancing them in subsequent stages, reflecting operational discretion consistent with system design. System logs capture scoring operations and recruitment campaign metadata but do not include recruiter override actions due to interface design that omits explicit override triggers. Consequently, automation bias management is addressed implicitly by human-driven workflows rather than critical awareness prompts or interpretive aids.

**Documentation of Personal Data Processing to Support Bias Detection and Correction**  
The system processing involves candidate personal data categories, including demographic and employment history details, which are processed strictly under data protection frameworks. Detailed records document the necessity of processing these data types for bias detection and correction in model training, fulfilling regulatory requirements. Data processing activities are logged to demonstrate why alternatives without special category data would fail to achieve equivalent bias mitigation, supporting auditability. Bias evaluation exercises conducted on validation sets indicate statistically significant reduction of disparate impact across gender and age segments, validated using fairness metrics such as demographic parity difference and equal opportunity difference, with thresholds pre-defined and met within the model retraining lifecycle. These measures are implemented by the provider in the development environment prior to market release; no real-time adaptive bias correction features are included in operational use for recruiters.
