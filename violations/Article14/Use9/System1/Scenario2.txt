[a] **Quotation:**  
"Human oversight shall aim to prevent or minimise the risks to health, safety or fundamental rights ... in particular where such risks persist despite the application of other requirements ..." (Article 14(2))  

[b] **Guideline:**  
Overseeing judges must be made aware of systemic limitations or biases in the AI’s outputs that could affect fundamental rights such as fair trial guarantees, and the system must explicitly support oversight measures that enable users to detect and mitigate these risks, especially under foreseeable misapplications like over-reliance on AI suggestions.  

[c] **Violation:**  
The system’s documentation and user interface do not include alerts or contextual warnings about the risks of automation bias, nor provide procedural recommendations for judges to verify AI-assisted conclusions against independent legal reasoning. Moreover, the system lacks features enabling users to simulate or test outcomes under alternative fact assumptions or legal interpretations, thereby reducing judges’ ability to identify and counteract potential risks to defendants' rights arising from uncritical acceptance of AI outputs.  

[d] **Justification:**  
This omission subtly breaches the requirement to minimize risks to fundamental rights because it fails to actively support oversight aimed at preventing automation bias and systemic errors. It is plausible because judicial professionals may not be trained to identify these risks autonomously without system-facilitated guidance, and such subtle gaps often go unnoticed in legal AI deployments.
